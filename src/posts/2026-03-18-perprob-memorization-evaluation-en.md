# PerProb: Indirectly Evaluating Memorization in Large Language Models

**Authors:** Yihan Liao, Jacky Keung, Xiaoxue Ma (Corresponding Author), Jingyu Zhang, Yicheng Sun
**Affiliations:** Department of Computer Science, City University of Hong Kong / Hong Kong Metropolitan University
**Keywords:** Large Language Model, Membership Inference Attack, Privacy-preserving, Perplexity, Log Probability

## One-Line Summary

This paper proposes **PerProb**, a label-free framework for indirectly evaluating LLM memorization vulnerabilities using perplexity and average log probability, and systematically evaluates four MIA attack patterns and three defense strategies.

---

## 1. Introduction

Large Language Models (LLMs) play a critical role in NLP, but their training on extensive datasets that may contain sensitive information raises serious privacy concerns. **Membership Inference Attack (MIA)** is one of the most prominent privacy risks, where an adversary attempts to determine whether specific data was part of a model's training set. The EU's GDPR considers the inference of training data membership as a privacy violation.

However, the effectiveness of MIA on LLMs remains controversial — some studies demonstrate successful attacks, while others argue MIAs perform only marginally better than random guessing. This inconsistency stems from two core challenges:

- **Challenge 1: Lack of membership labels.** Most LLMs (e.g., GPT series) do not disclose their pretraining data, making it infeasible to obtain ground-truth labels.
- **Challenge 2: Generalization vs. memorization.** LLMs are trained to generalize over vast corpora, making individual memorized examples harder to distinguish.

### Key Contributions

1. **PerProb framework:** A label-free framework for indirectly evaluating LLM memorization based on perplexity (PPL) and average log probability (λ(W)) in generation tasks.
2. **Unified assessment under four MIA attack patterns:** Demonstrating PerProb's effectiveness across models and datasets in both black-box and white-box settings.
3. **Defense evaluation:** Assessing Knowledge Distillation (KD), Early Stopping (ES), and Differential Privacy (DP) for reducing privacy leakage.

## 2. Background

### Comparison of MIA Methods

| Method | Assumptions | Required Access | Limitations in LLMs |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Confidence-based | Labeled member/non-member data | Output probabilities (black-box) | Confidence often indistinguishable due to LLM generalization |
| Loss-based | Lower loss on member samples | Loss scores or logits | Loss values unstable in LLMs |
| Gradient-based | Model gradient access | Parameters and gradients (white-box) | Impractical for large API-access LLMs |
| Shadow model | Train on similar data | Data generator and architecture | Requires labeled data; data mismatch issues |
| Prompt-based | Calibrated prompts | Model responses to crafted prompts | Performance depends on prompt design |
| **PerProb (Proposed)** | **No label or internal access** | **Only model outputs; both generation and classification** | Indirect inference; assumes behavioral differences from training-induced memory |

## 3. Methodology

### 3.1 Target Models

- **GPT-2:** Undisclosed pretraining set → represents scenarios with no ground-truth labels
- **GPT-Neo (1.3B, 2.7B):** Trained primarily on The Pile → intermediate transparency scenario with partial training data availability

### 3.2 PerProb for Generation Tasks

PerProb evaluates differences in two metrics between shadow and victim model outputs:

**Perplexity (PPL):**

PPL(W) = exp(−(1/N) × Σ log p_θ(w_i | w_{<i}))

**Average log probability (λ(W)):**

λ(W) = (1/N) × Σ log p_θ(w_i | w_{<i})

**Key intuition:** If a model has memorized specific training data, it assigns higher likelihoods to that data, resulting in lower perplexity and higher average log-probability:
- Memorized data: lower PPL, higher λ(W)
- Non-memorized data: higher PPL, lower λ(W)

PerProb compares PPL and λ(W) for data generated under identical prompts before and after training, estimating training-induced memory without requiring membership labels.

### 3.3 Classification Tasks

For classification tasks, ground-truth labels enable direct MIA evaluation. **Random Forest (RF)** and **Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP)** serve as attack models. An attack is considered successful if the F1-score exceeds 50% (random guessing).

## 4. Threat Model Taxonomy

MIA is categorized into four distinct attack patterns based on black-box and white-box frameworks.

![Figure 1: Structure of four attack patterns on generation and classification tasks](/images/papers/perprob-memorization/overview2.png)
*Figure 1: Structure of four attack patterns. Adversary 1 and 2 use shadow datasets, with Adversary 2 sharing victim model parameters. Adversary 3 combines auxiliary and shadow datasets. Adversary 4 uses partial victim datasets.*

### Adversary 1 (Black-box)
- Untrained LLM as shadow model (S), trained on shadow dataset (D_shadow) mirroring victim dataset distribution
- Most realistic and challenging scenario for adversaries

### Adversary 2 (White-box)
- S shares parameters with victim model (V)
- Most severe scenario: full compromise of V's parameters

### Adversary 3 (Black-box)
- Combines auxiliary dataset (D_aux) with 10% of D_shadow for training S
- Limited overlap with V's training data distribution

### Adversary 4 (White-box)
- Adversary has access to a subset of D_victim for training S
- Partial data leakage scenario with thresholds from 0.1 to 0.5

## 5. Experimental Setup

### 5.1 Datasets

Five publicly available text datasets:

| Dataset | Description | Scale |
|---------|-------------|-------|
| IMDB | Polarized movie reviews, balanced positive/negative | 50,000 |
| Agnews | News articles, 4 categories | 2,000+ sources |
| 20Newsgroup | Documents from 20 newsgroups | 18,846 |
| Bank77 | Online banking queries, avg. length 56 | 13,083 |
| WOS (Web of Science) | Academic papers, 134 subcategories | 46,985 |

### 5.2 Generation Task Setup

- **Datasets:** IMDB, Agnews, WOS (diverse text types serving as mutual D_aux in Adversary 3)
- Tailored prompts to generate **1,000 texts per dataset** from original LLMs (D_ori)
- Each adversary generates its own dataset (D_ad1–D_ad4)
- Adversary 3 pairings: IMDB↔Agnews, IMDB↔WOS, Agnews↔WOS

### 5.3 Classification Task Setup

- All five datasets used, focused on GPT-2
- RF: 100 estimators for simpler datasets, 200 for complex ones (Bank77, WOS)
- MLP: 3 hidden layers for simpler datasets, 4 for complex ones

### 5.4 Model Parameters

- Generation tasks: 10 epochs, learning rate 1e-6 (to prevent gradient explosion)
- Classification tasks: RF and MLP configured per dataset characteristics

**Key Findings:**

- **Adversary 1 & 2 (classic black/white-box):** Generally show higher λ(W) and lower PPL — closer to V's distribution
- **Adversary 3 & 4:** Exhibit higher PPL and more instances of λ(W) = −∞
- **Model size effect:** Larger models (GPT-Neo 2.7B) produce more generated data with λ(W) = −∞ and PPL = ∞, suggesting they have not memorized training data significantly and retain strong generalization capabilities
- **Dataset characteristics:** IMDB and Agnews show consistent results due to well-defined semantic structures, while WOS shows more pronounced variations due to its 100+ subcategories
- **Role of D_aux in Adversary 3:** Semantically similar D_aux (e.g., 20Newsgroup for Agnews) yields lower PPL and higher λ(W), enhancing attack effectiveness

> **Finding 1:** The effectiveness of MIA depends on training datasets and the generative capabilities of LLMs. Larger models exhibit stronger generalization and resistance to overfitting, while smaller models are more sensitive to MIA.

## 7. Defenses

### 7.1 Techniques

- **Knowledge Distillation (KD):** Transfer from teacher (GPT-Neo 2.7B) to student (GPT-2 medium, GPT-Neo 1.3B). Temperature 2, minimizing KL divergence loss.
- **Early Stopping (ES):** Halt training when PPL decrement falls below threshold (0.001–0.01) in consecutive steps.
- **Differential Privacy (DP):** Inject Laplace noise into output posteriors. Improved DP uses maximum posterior value as noise mean (μ). Privacy budget ε = 0.5, 1, 2.

### 7.2 Generation Task Defense Results

**KD and ES efficacy on GPT-2:**

| Dataset | Adv.1 PPL | KD PPL | ES PPL | Adv.1 λ(W) | KD λ(W) | ES λ(W) |
|---------|-----------|--------|--------|-------------|---------|---------|
| IMDB | 252.22 | 201.83 | **478.23** | -18.56 | **-23.5** | -19.52 |
| Agnews | 1149.91 | 205.35 | **1483.73** | -38.76 | **-51.32** | -35.84 |
| WOS | 240.17 | 179.23 | 245.27 | -24.62 | **-29.25** | **-26.49** |

- **ES:** Superior at elevating PPL (prevents overfitting by halting training early)
- **KD:** Superior at lowering λ(W) (transfers generalized knowledge, reducing model confidence)
- **Nuanced limitation of KD in Adversary 3:** When D_aux is semantically similar to D_victim, KD may inadvertently improve alignment between S and V, reducing PPL

> **Finding 3:** Both KD and ES effectively mitigate MIA risks in LLMs. ES shows superior performance in elevating PPL, while KD performs better in lowering λ(W). The importance of selecting appropriate defense strategies against different attack patterns is highlighted.

### 7.3 Classification Task Defense Results (Differential Privacy)

**DP F1-score comparison (selected results):**

| DP μ | ε | IMDB Adv.1 | Agnews Adv.1 | 20News Adv.1 | Bank77 Adv.1 | WOS Adv.1 |
|------|---|------------|--------------|--------------|--------------|-----------|
| 0 | 0.5 | 0.644 | **0.706** | 0.604 | 0.725 | 0.549 |
| Max posterior | 0.5 | **0.626** | 0.714 | **0.572** | 0.722 | **0.525** |
| Original | - | 0.652 | 0.765 | 0.711 | 0.753 | 0.689 |

- DP most effective on 20Newsgroup and WOS (12.43% and 16.15% improvement respectively)
- **Improved DP (μ = max posterior) consistently outperforms traditional DP**, especially on datasets with high category complexity
- Lower privacy budgets (smaller ε) amplify DP effectiveness, with potential trade-offs in model performance

![](/images/papers/perprob-memorization/dp_bank.png) ![](/images/papers/perprob-memorization/dp_wos.png)

## 8. Limitations

1. **Model scale constraints:** Evaluation focuses on mid-sized models (GPT-2, GPT-Neo). The core design of PerProb is model-independent and applicable to API-only or black-box LLMs, but evaluation on larger-scale or closed-source LLMs remains future work.
2. **Prompt consistency assumption:** PerProb assumes consistent prompts between shadow and victim models. Prompt variance in real-world deployments may affect metric stability.
3. **Limited attack model diversity:** Only RF and MLP are used as attack models. Extensions to GBDT, neural attackers, and other classification tasks (summarization, code generation) could further validate generality.
4. **No direct comparison with prior MIA methods:** Prior methods require labeled data or internal access that does not align with real-world LLM evaluation constraints.

---

## 9. Conclusion

PerProb provides a practical, label-free framework for evaluating LLM memorization through PPL and λ(W). Key takeaways:

- **Larger models (GPT-Neo 2.7B)** exhibit stronger generalization and inherent resistance to memorization
- **Smaller models (GPT-2)** show higher susceptibility to overfitting and direct memorization
- **Defense strategies:** KD excels at lowering λ(W), ES at elevating PPL, and DP is effective in black-box settings or datasets with high category complexity
- **KD's nuanced limitation:** May inadvertently improve alignment between shadow and victim models when semantically similar auxiliary datasets are used
- The findings emphasize the importance of selecting defense strategies based on attack patterns and suggest combined potential of KD, ES, and DP
